Government House

Government House is one of the most historic buildings in Port Louis, the capital city of Mauritius. Dubbed as the oldest building of the capital, it was built during the French colonisation era and served as the residence of the first French governor of Mauritius. The construction of this building was supervised by the governor himself, and its architecture is typical of the period.


One of the most striking features of the Government House is the statue of Queen Victoria that stands in front of the building. The statue was erected during the British colonial period and is a testament to the island's colonial history. The statue portrays Queen Victoria with an air of un-amused indifference, which is quite characteristic of her personality.

The Parliament of Mauritius

The Parliament of Mauritius, established in 1968, is the nation's supreme legislative body. Operating under a unicameral system, it consists of the National Assembly, where elected Members of Parliament (MPs) represent constituencies across the island. The Parliament is responsible for enacting laws, approving budgets, and overseeing the government's work. Located in Port Louis, the Parliament building symbolises the country's commitment to democracy. It is a place where key decisions shaping Mauritius's future are debated and made, reflecting the voices and concerns of its people.


The government of Mauritius is currently being led by the Prime Minister, Pravind Kumar Jugnauth, from the Movement Socialist Militant (MSM) party, who has been in office since December 10 2014.

History of Mauritius

Mauritius has a rich and diverse history, with influences from Dutch, French, and British colonisers. The island was first discovered by Arab sailors in the 9th century, but it remained uninhabited until the arrival of the Portuguese in the 16th century. The Dutch were the first to establish a settlement in 1638, but they abandoned it in 1710 due to harsh living conditions.

The French took control of the island in 1715 and developed the sugar industry, which became a cornerstone of the island's economy. In 1810, the British captured Mauritius, and it remained a British colony until it gained independence in 1968. The legacy of these various cultures can be seen in the island's architecture, cuisine, and traditions.

Today, Mauritius is known for its political stability, vibrant culture, and as a melting pot of different ethnicities and religions. The history of Mauritius is a testament to its resilience and ability to adapt to changing circumstances.

Independence Day: March 12 1968




Mauritius achieved independence from British rule on March 12, 1968, with Sir Seewoosagur Ramgoolam becoming the island's first Prime Minister. As a key figure in the independence movement, Ramgoolam is honoured as the "Father of the Nation." This pivotal moment marked the transition from colonial governance to self-rule, and Independence Day is now celebrated with national pride, including parades, cultural events, and reflections on Mauritius's journey to sovereignty.

Dodo

The dodo, an iconic symbol of Mauritius, was a flightless bird endemic to the island. Discovered by Portuguese sailors in the late 16th century, the dodo stood about one metre tall and had a robust, grey body with a distinctive beak. Unfortunately, due to over-hunting by sailors and the introduction of non-native animals, the dodo became extinct by the late 17th century. Despite its extinction, the dodo remains a symbol of Mauritius&s unique natural heritage and a poignant reminder of the impact of human activities on wildlife.

Mauritius Kestrel

The Mauritius kestrel is the national bird of Mauritius, symbolising the island&s dedication to wildlife conservation. This small, agile raptor was once critically endangered due to habitat loss and introduced predators. However, through intensive conservation efforts, including habitat restoration and captive breeding programs, its population has significantly recovered. The Mauritius kestrel is known for its distinctive plumage and impressive hunting skills, and it now serves as a symbol of hope and resilience in the ongoing efforts to protect and preserve the island&s unique biodiversity.

Early Settlements of Mauritius

Portuguese Exploration

The island of Mauritius was first discovered by Portuguese sailors in 1506. Although they were the first Europeans to arrive, the Portuguese did not establish any settlements. Their main interest was in navigating trade routes, and Mauritius remained largely uninhabited during their brief exploration. Despite this, their discovery marked the beginning of European interest in the island.


Dutch Colonisation

The Dutch were the first to attempt settlement on Mauritius, establishing a colony in 1638. They built a small settlement and began cultivating crops, but their efforts were hindered by the island&s harsh climate, diseases, and frequent cyclones. The Dutch left Mauritius in 1710, abandoning their settlement. However, their brief presence left a lasting mark on the island's botanical history, including the introduction of sugarcane.


French Rule

In 1710, the French East India Company took control of Mauritius, renaming it Île de France. The French developed the island&s economy, particularly focusing on agriculture and the sugar industry. They introduced enslaved Africans and indentured labou rers from India to work on the sugar plantations. The French era was marked by significant development in infrastructure and the establishment of a robust plantation economy that greatly influenced Mauritius's socio-economic landscape.


British Colonial Period

The British captured Mauritius in 1810 during the Napoleonic Wars, and the island officially became a British colony in 1814 under the Treaty of Paris. The British administration continued the development of the sugar industry and implemented various administrative reforms. The introduction of Indian labourers as indentured servants significantly influenced the island&s demographic and cultural composition. Mauritius remained under British rule until it gained independence in 1968, and the legacy of British governance can still be seen in the island&s legal and educational systems.
